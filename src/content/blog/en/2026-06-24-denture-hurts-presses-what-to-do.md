## Removable Denture Hurts and Presses — What to Do in Tallinn: Advice from Dr Dmitri Sonin

You put in your new denture in the morning, and by lunchtime your gum is already sore and red. Sound familiar? You're not alone — pressure pain and irritation from removable dentures is one of the most common complaints Dr Dmitri Sonin hears at Sonin Dental Clinic clinic in Tallinn. The good news: in most cases, the problem has a clear cause and a straightforward solution.

---

### Why Does a Removable Denture Cause Pain and Pressure?

A denture doesn't sit in a static environment. Your gum tissue and underlying bone change shape over time — and the denture doesn't always keep up. Here are the most common mechanical reasons for discomfort:

- **Bone resorption.** After tooth extraction, the jaw ridge loses volume — roughly 25% of bone width in the first year alone. A denture fitted 2–3 years ago may no longer match the ridge it was made for, creating uneven pressure points.
- **Uneven occlusion (bite).** If the denture is even 0.5–1 mm off in one spot, chewing forces concentrate there and cause a sore spot within hours of use.
- **Sharp edges or clasps.** Acrylic can develop micro-fractures or rough edges that act like sandpaper against soft tissue.
- **Poorly adjusted fit on first delivery.** No denture comes out of the lab perfect. Most patients need 2–4 adjustment appointments in the first month — this is completely normal and expected.
- **Dry mouth or medication side effects.** Reduced saliva means less natural cushioning between the denture base and the mucosa.

Understanding the cause is step one. Without it, grinding down random spots yourself only makes things worse.

---

### What to Do Right Now: Step-by-Step

1. **Remove the denture for at least 6–8 hours.** Give the irritated tissue a chance to recover. Sleeping without the denture is a good habit anyway.
2. **Rinse with warm saline solution** (1 teaspoon of salt per 200 ml water) 3–4 times a day. This reduces inflammation without masking the real problem.
3. **Do not use over-the-counter adhesive creams as a long-term fix.** Denture adhesive can temporarily mask a poor fit, allowing damage to accumulate unnoticed under the surface.
4. **Mark the pain spot if you can.** Before your appointment, wear the denture for 30–60 minutes, then mark the sore area with a clean lip liner or ask the dental assistant to help. This helps the dentist identify the exact pressure point in seconds.
5. **Book a correction appointment within 3–5 days.** A correction appointment typically takes 20–30 minutes and involves selective grinding of the pressure point — you'll feel relief the same day.

---

### When to See a Dentist Immediately

Most denture pain is mechanical and not urgent. However, contact Sonin Hambaravi clinic in Tallinn without delay if you notice:

- **A sore that doesn't heal within 10–14 days** after removing the denture — persistent ulcers need to be assessed to rule out serious tissue changes.
- **Swelling, pus, or a foul taste** — signs of possible infection that require prompt treatment.
- **The denture has cracked or broken.** A fractured denture can have sharp edges that cut the mucosa deeply.
- **Difficulty swallowing or breathing** — extremely rare, but warrants emergency care immediately.

---

### Prevention and Key Takeaways

Dr Dmitri Sonin recommends treating denture adjustments not as a sign that something went wrong, but as a scheduled part of the process.

- **Plan for 2–4 correction visits** in the first 4–6 weeks after receiving a new denture.
- **Schedule a professional check-up every 12 months** even if you feel no discomfort — silent bone loss can ruin a good fit quietly.
- **Clean the denture twice daily** with a soft brush and denture-specific cleaner, not toothpaste (too abrasive).
- **Have the fit re-evaluated every 3–5 years** or sooner if you notice looseness or new pressure spots.

A well-fitting denture should feel comfortable for 6–8 hours of daily wear. If it doesn't, that's a clinical problem worth solving — not something to endure in silence.
