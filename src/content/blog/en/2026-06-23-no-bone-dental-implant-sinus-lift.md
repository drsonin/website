## Not Enough Bone for a Dental Implant? Dr Dmitri Sonin Explains Sinus Lift and Bone Graft Options in Tallinn

You've been told you want dental implants — but then comes the news that stops many patients in their tracks: *"I'm sorry, you don't have enough bone."* Maybe you lost a tooth years ago, or you've worn dentures for a long time. Whatever the reason, hearing this can feel like a dead end. The good news? It isn't. As Dr Dmitri Sonin sees regularly at Sonin Dental Clinic clinic in Tallinn, the vast majority of patients who lack sufficient bone can still receive implants — they simply need one extra step first.

---

### Why Do Patients Lose Bone — and What Does That Actually Mean?

When a natural tooth is removed, the jawbone beneath it begins to resorb. This is not a complication — it is normal biology. Without the pressure and stimulation that a tooth root provides, the bone simply shrinks. Studies show that up to **40–60% of bone volume** can be lost in the first two to three years after an extraction.

In the upper jaw, there is an additional challenge: the maxillary sinuses. These air-filled cavities sit directly above the upper back teeth. When bone resorbs downward and the sinus expands downward, the remaining bone height between the jaw ridge and the sinus floor can shrink to just **2–4 mm** — far too little for a standard implant, which typically requires at least **10–12 mm**.

Common causes of insufficient bone include:

- Long-term tooth loss or delayed implant placement
- Periodontal (gum) disease, which destroys supporting bone
- Trauma or infection
- Extended use of full or partial dentures

---

### What Are the Solutions? Sinus Lift and Bone Grafting Explained

This is where modern implant dentistry steps in. At Sonin Hambaravi, Dr Dmitri Sonin uses two well-established procedures to rebuild the bone volume needed for a secure, lasting implant.

**Bone Grafting**
A bone graft adds volume to areas where the jaw ridge has shrunk. The graft material — which can come from your own body, a donor source, or a synthetic substitute — acts as a scaffold. Over **4–6 months**, your own bone cells grow into this scaffold, creating new, solid bone that can support an implant.

**Sinus Lift (Sinus Augmentation)**
When the upper back jaw lacks height due to sinus proximity, a sinus lift procedure gently lifts the sinus membrane and places graft material into the newly created space beneath it. Depending on the amount of bone present before the procedure:

- **Lateral window sinus lift** — used when bone height is under 4–5 mm; healing takes approximately 6–9 months before implant placement
- **Internal (crestal) sinus lift** — suitable when 4–6 mm of bone remains; can sometimes be combined with implant placement on the same day

Both procedures are performed under local anaesthesia and are typically very well tolerated by patients. Mild swelling and discomfort for a few days afterwards is normal and manageable with standard pain relief.

### When Should You See a Dentist Without Delay?

If you have experienced any of the following, do not wait to book an assessment:

- A tooth was extracted **more than six months ago** and no implant has been placed yet — bone loss is already progressing
- You notice your denture becoming loose — this signals ongoing bone resorption
- You have been told by another clinic that implants are "not possible" for you — always seek a second opinion with an implantologist
- You experience sinus pressure, congestion, or discomfort in the upper jaw — this may indicate sinus-related bone changes

Early evaluation means more options and shorter treatment timelines.

---

### Key Takeaways: What Patients Should Know

- Lack of bone does **not** automatically rule out implants — it means you need a preparatory procedure first
- Sinus lifts and bone grafts have **decades of clinical evidence** behind them with success rates above 95%
- Treatment timelines range from **4 to 12 months** depending on the extent of bone rebuilding required
- The sooner you act after tooth loss, the simpler and shorter the treatment usually is
- Results, when achieved, are permanent — implants placed after bone grafting last just as long as those placed in naturally adequate bone
