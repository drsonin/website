### What Counts as a Dental Emergency?

Not every toothache requires an emergency visit, but some situations genuinely cannot wait. You should seek urgent dental care if you experience:

- **Severe or throbbing tooth pain** that does not respond to over-the-counter pain relief
- **A knocked-out or partially dislodged tooth** following trauma or an accident
- **A broken, cracked, or fractured tooth** causing sharp pain or sensitivity
- **A lost filling or crown** leaving the tooth exposed and vulnerable
- **Dental abscess or swelling** — a potentially serious infection that can spread
- **Bleeding gums or soft tissue injuries** that do not stop within a short time
- **A broken dental appliance** causing irritation or injury to the mouth

If you are unsure whether your situation qualifies as an emergency, it is always better to call us. Our team will help assess your symptoms and advise you on the fastest path to relief.

### Why Prompt Treatment Matters

Delaying treatment for a dental emergency can turn a manageable problem into a far more complex — and costly — one. An untreated abscess, for example, can spread infection to the jaw, neck, or even the bloodstream. A knocked-out tooth has the best chance of being reimplanted if treated within 30 to 60 minutes of the injury.

Dr Dmitri Sonin and the Sonin Hambaravi team prioritise emergency cases to ensure you receive timely care. Acting quickly not only relieves pain faster — it often preserves your natural tooth and prevents the need for more extensive procedures down the line.

### What to Do Before You Reach the Clinic

While you make your way to our Tallinn clinic, there are a few steps that can help protect your tooth and manage discomfort:

- **For a knocked-out tooth:** Handle it by the crown, not the root. Rinse gently with water if dirty, and try to place it back in the socket. If that is not possible, store it in a small container of milk or saliva — not water.
- **For pain and swelling:** Apply a cold compress to the outside of the cheek in short intervals to reduce swelling. Avoid placing aspirin directly on the gum.
- **For a lost crown or filling:** Dental cement (available at pharmacies) can provide temporary protection. Avoid chewing on that side.
- **For bleeding:** Apply gentle pressure with clean gauze or a cloth.

These are temporary measures only — professional care should follow as soon as possible.
