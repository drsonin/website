## My Tooth Broke After a Root Canal — Implant or Bridge? Dr Dmitri Sonin Answers

Maria, a 42-year-old teacher from Tallinn, came to us last spring with a familiar story: she'd had a root canal on her upper molar years ago, crowned it, and thought the problem was solved for good. Then one morning, while eating breakfast, she heard a crack — and the tooth was gone below the gumline. If this sounds like your situation, you're not alone. And you do have clear, reliable options.

---

### Why Do Root Canal Teeth Break More Easily?

This is one of the most common questions Dr Dmitri Sonin hears at Sonin Dental Clinic clinic, and the answer is straightforward once you understand the biology.

During root canal treatment, the nerve and blood supply inside the tooth are removed. This process saves the tooth from infection, but it also leaves the remaining structure significantly more brittle over time — essentially, the tooth "dries out" at a microscopic level. Without the internal pulp providing moisture and nutrients, the dentine becomes more prone to fracture under normal chewing forces.

Add to that the reality that a tooth needing a root canal was usually already weakened by decay or a large filling beforehand. If a full ceramic or metal-ceramic crown wasn't placed promptly after the procedure, or if the crown was older and worn, the risk of catastrophic fracture increases considerably. Studies suggest that un-crowned root canal teeth are up to six times more likely to fracture than crowned ones. Even with a crown, if the fracture line runs below the bone level — a so-called subgingival fracture — restoration becomes very complicated or impossible.

---

### Implant or Bridge: Which Option Is Right for You?

This is the key decision, and it depends on several clinical factors. Here is how Dmitri Sonin approaches it in practice:

**Dental Implant**
- Recommended when the root cannot be saved and bone volume is adequate
- A titanium post is placed into the jawbone, healing takes **3–6 months**
- The final crown is then fitted on top — the result looks and functions like a natural tooth
- Implants preserve bone and do not involve the neighbouring teeth
- Success rate: **95–98% over 10 years** when placed and maintained correctly
- Best long-term investment for most patients under 65 in good general health

**Dental Bridge**
- A good choice when implant surgery is not suitable (thin bone, health contraindications, or patient preference)
- The two teeth adjacent to the gap are prepared and used as support pillars
- Treatment time: typically **2–3 weeks** from tooth extraction to the final bridge
- More affordable upfront, but requires healthy neighbouring teeth to be ground down
- Does not prevent bone loss under the gap over time

In straightforward cases, an implant is usually the first recommendation at our clinic. However, if the neighbouring teeth already have large restorations or crowns, a bridge can be a perfectly sensible and durable solution.

---

### When Should You See a Dentist Immediately?

Do not wait if you notice any of the following after a tooth breaks:

- **Sharp pain** or pressure sensitivity in the area
- **Swelling** of the gum, cheek, or jaw — this may indicate infection spreading from the old root
- **Bleeding** that doesn't stop within 20–30 minutes
- **Fever or a bad taste** in the mouth, which can signal an abscess

A broken root canal tooth can harbour bacteria even without obvious pain, because the nerve is gone. The absence of pain does not mean the situation is not urgent. Contact your dentist within **24–48 hours** — sooner if any swelling is present.

---

### Key Takeaways: What You Can Do Right Now

1. **Keep the area clean** — brush gently and rinse with saltwater or chlorhexidine mouthwash
2. **Avoid chewing on that side** until you've been examined
3. **Don't delay the consultation** — the longer a gap remains untreated, the more bone you lose, which can complicate or increase the cost of an implant later
4. **Ask about a temporary solution** — your dentist can often place a temporary restoration while you plan the definitive treatment
