### What Counts as a Dental Emergency?

Many patients are unsure whether their situation truly requires urgent attention. As a general rule, if you are experiencing significant pain, visible damage, swelling, or bleeding, you should seek emergency dental care as soon as possible.

Common dental emergencies include:

- **Severe toothache** — persistent or throbbing pain that does not respond to over-the-counter painkillers
- **Knocked-out or displaced tooth** — time is critical here; the sooner you are treated, the better the chances of saving the tooth
- **Cracked or broken tooth** — especially when sharp edges cause discomfort or soft tissue injury
- **Lost filling or crown** — leaving a tooth exposed can lead to rapid sensitivity and further damage
- **Dental abscess** — a bacterial infection that can spread if left untreated
- **Soft tissue injuries** — cuts or lacerations to the gums, cheeks, or lips

When in doubt, contact us. It is always better to check than to wait and risk a more serious problem developing.

### Why Prompt Treatment Matters

Ignoring a dental emergency is rarely a neutral choice. Pain can escalate, infection can spread to surrounding teeth or even the jaw, and what might have been a simple procedure can become far more complex and costly. **Dr Dmitri Sonin** and the team at Sonin Hambaravi have seen firsthand how timely intervention saves both teeth and patients from prolonged discomfort.

For example, a tooth that has been knocked out has the best chance of being successfully reimplanted within 30 to 60 minutes of the injury. Similarly, an abscess left untreated can develop into a serious systemic infection requiring hospitalisation. Acting quickly is not an overreaction — it is simply good dental sense.

### What to Do While You Wait for Your Appointment

In the time between a dental emergency occurring and reaching our Tallinn clinic, there are a few practical steps that can help:

- **For a knocked-out tooth:** Keep it moist. Place it back in the socket if possible, or store it in milk or between your cheek and gum. Do not rinse it with water or let it dry out.
- **For a toothache:** Rinse gently with warm salt water and avoid applying aspirin directly to the gum.
- **For a cracked tooth:** Avoid hard foods and extreme temperatures until you are seen by a dentist.
- **For swelling:** Apply a cold compress to the outside of the cheek for 10–15 minutes at a time.

These measures are temporary. They do not replace professional treatment.
