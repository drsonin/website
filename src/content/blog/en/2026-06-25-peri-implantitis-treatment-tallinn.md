Dental implants have transformed modern dentistry, giving millions of patients a permanent, natural-looking solution for missing teeth. But like natural teeth, implants are not immune to disease. One of the most serious — and increasingly common — complications is peri-implantitis: an inflammatory condition affecting the gum and bone tissue surrounding a dental implant. Understanding what it is, why it happens, and how to respond quickly can mean the difference between saving your implant and losing it altogether.

## Peri-Implantitis: What Every Implant Patient Should Know — Explained by Dr Dmitri Sonin

You noticed some redness around your implant crown a few weeks ago, brushed it off as minor irritation, and now the gum looks swollen and feels tender when you chew. Sound familiar? This scenario arrives in the consultation chair at Sonin Dental Clinic more often than most patients expect — and the sooner it is addressed, the better the outcome.

### What Is Peri-Implantitis and Why Does It Happen?

Peri-implantitis is a bacterial infection-driven inflammation that attacks the soft tissue and the supporting bone around a dental implant. It is similar in mechanism to periodontitis around natural teeth, but it tends to progress faster and cause more rapid bone loss — sometimes destroying 1–2 mm of bone per year if left untreated.

The primary cause is a biofilm of bacteria (dental plaque) that accumulates on the implant surface below the gum line. Several factors increase the risk significantly:

- **Poor oral hygiene** — irregular brushing and flossing allow plaque to calcify into tartar around the implant collar
- **History of periodontal disease** — patients who have lost teeth due to gum disease carry the same bacterial profile that threatens implants
- **Smoking** — smokers face up to three times the risk of peri-implantitis compared to non-smokers, as tobacco impairs blood circulation and immune response in gum tissue
- **Uncontrolled diabetes** — elevated blood sugar compromises the body's ability to fight infection around implants
- **Excess cement** — residual dental cement left after crown placement can trigger an immediate inflammatory response under the gum
- **Poorly fitted prosthetics** — crowns or bridges that do not fit precisely create plaque traps that are nearly impossible to clean at home

Dr Dmitri Sonin emphasises that implant surface characteristics also play a role: the roughened titanium surfaces that promote bone integration can, paradoxically, also harbour bacteria more readily than smooth enamel.

### What Should You Do If You Suspect Peri-Implantitis?

Do not wait and see. If you notice any warning signs, take these concrete steps:

1. **Improve your home hygiene immediately.** Use an interdental brush sized to fit the gap around your implant, a water flosser set to medium pressure, and a soft-bristle toothbrush twice daily.
2. **Use an antimicrobial mouthwash.** Chlorhexidine 0.12–0.2% rinse for 30 seconds twice daily can reduce bacterial load within 1–2 weeks, but it is a bridge measure — not a cure.
3. **Book a professional assessment within 1–2 weeks.** A dentist will measure the probing depth around the implant (healthy is ≤3–4 mm), take a periapical X-ray to assess bone levels, and determine the stage of infection.

Early-stage peri-implantitis (peri-implant mucositis — inflammation limited to soft tissue only) is fully reversible with professional cleaning and improved hygiene. Once bone loss begins, surgical intervention is often required.

### When Should You See a Dentist Immediately?

Seek urgent dental care — ideally within 24–48 hours — if you experience:

- **Spontaneous bleeding** from the gum around the implant without any provocation
- **Visible pus or discharge** from the implant site
- **Significant mobility** of the implant crown or the implant itself
- **Acute pain** when biting, combined with visible gum swelling

These signs indicate advanced infection. According to Dr Dmitri Sonin, patients who present at this stage frequently require bone grafting procedures alongside implant debridement, and in the most severe cases, implant removal becomes unavoidable.

### Prevention and Key Takeaways

The most effective treatment for peri-implantitis is preventing it in the first place. Sonin Hambaravi follows a structured implant maintenance protocol:

- **Professional cleaning every 6 months** using instruments specifically designed not to scratch titanium surfaces
- **Annual radiographic bone-level check** to detect early loss before symptoms appear
- **Smoking cessation support** — quitting within the first year post-implant dramatically improves long-term survival rates
- **Diabetes management** in coordination with your GP before and after implant placement

A dental implant can last 20–30 years with proper care. Peri-implantitis, caught early and treated correctly at Sonin Dental Clinic, does not have to end that story prematurely.
